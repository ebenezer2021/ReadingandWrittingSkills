SUMMARY OF LUCA

Luca is a 2021 animated film by Pixar and Disney, directed by Enrico Casarosa. The movie is a coming of age adventure set in a beautiful seaside town on the Italian Riviera.

Luca Paguro is a curious and timid boy who has spent his whole life in the safety of his underwater home. But he’s not just any boy he’s a sea monster, and he’s been taught to fear the world above. That is, until he meets Alberto, a fearless and mischievous boy who introduces him to a life of adventure on land. There, Luca discovers something incredible when he steps out of the water, he transforms into a human.

At first, it’s all about fun: running through the sunlit streets of Portorosso, riding makeshift Vespas, and dreaming about exploring the world. Then they meet Giulia, a strong willed girl who invites them to team up for the town’s annual race against the arrogant bully, Ercole. But as Luca grows closer to Giulia, Alberto starts to feel left behind, leading to a painful falling out.

When Alberto reveals his true sea monster form in a moment of frustration, Luca, afraid of being exposed, pretends not to know him. Hurt and betrayed, Alberto disappears. But when it starts to rain during the race threatening to reveal Luca’s secret to the whole town he makes a choice. Instead of running, he embraces who he is, standing by Alberto’s side. To their surprise, Giulia and even some of the townspeople accept them for who they are.

In the end, Luca follows his dream of going to school, while Alberto finds a new home with Giulia’s father. Their goodbye is bittersweet, but their friendship remains unbreakable. Luca is a heartwarming tale about stepping out of your comfort zone, embracing who you are, and finding the people who truly accept you.



SUMMARY OF PONYO

Ponyo is a 2008 animated fantasy film directed by Hayao Miyazaki and produced by Studio Ghibli. Inspired by The Little Mermaid, the film tells a heartwarming story of a magical goldfish named Ponyo who longs to become human after forming a bond with a young boy named Sōsuke.

Ponyo isn’t just any fish she’s a curious, free spirited little creature with a mind of her own. Living under the sea with her overprotective father, Fujimoto, she dreams of seeing the world above. One day, she sneaks away and ends up in the hands of Sōsuke, a kind and gentle boy who lives by the ocean. He names her Ponyo, and in that moment, an unbreakable bond forms between them.

But Ponyo’s disappearance throws the ocean into chaos. Fujimoto desperately tries to bring her back, fearing the power she holds. Meanwhile, Ponyo’s love for Sōsuke is so strong that she begins to change growing arms, legs, and transforming into a human girl. The problem? Her magic is too powerful, and her transformation causes a massive storm, flooding the town and threatening to throw nature off balance.

As the waves crash around them, Sōsuke and Ponyo set off on a journey to fix what’s been broken. Along the way, they face challenges that test their trust in each other. When they finally reach Granmamare, the great sea goddess and Ponyo’s mother, she presents a choice: If Sōsuke truly loves Ponyo for who she is—whether as a fish or a girl—she can remain human forever. Without hesitation, Sōsuke promises to care for her, sealing their bond and restoring balance to the world.

In the end, Ponyo gets her wish to live as a human, and Sōsuke gains a friend who will always bring magic into his life. Ponyo is a heartwarming story about love, trust, and the beauty of childhood wonder wrapped in breathtaking animation and Studio Ghibli’s signature charm.

SIMILARITIES

Though Luca (2021) and Ponyo (2008) come from different studios Pixar and Studio Ghibli, respectively they share several striking similarities in themes, characters, and storytelling.

Both movies center around magical sea creatures (Luca as a sea monster and Ponyo as a goldfish-like being) who transform into human children. The ocean plays a major role in both films, serving as both a home and a source of mystery, adventure, and change.

Luca struggles between staying in his safe underwater home or pursuing his dream of living on land. His parents try to stop him, fearing for his safety. Ponyo faces a similar conflict her father, Fujimoto, wants her to stay in the sea, believing humans are dangerous. Both movies explore themes of self discovery, freedom, and breaking away from parental fears.
